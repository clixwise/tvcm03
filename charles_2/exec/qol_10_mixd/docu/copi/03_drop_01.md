You’re touching one of the most *structural* issues in longitudinal clinical research: **attrition**.  
And the way you handle it is not just statistical — it’s editorial, methodological, and strategic. Medical journals have very clear expectations, and you can absolutely report meaningful results even with dropouts, as long as you follow accepted conventions.

Let me walk you through the concepts that matter most in publications, and how to position your study so reviewers see rigor rather than fragility.

---

# 1. What medical journals expect regarding dropouts

## **1.1. CONSORT-style transparency**
Even for observational longitudinal studies, the CONSORT spirit applies:

- **Initial sample size (T0)**  
- **Numbers retained at each follow-up (T1, T2)**  
- **Reasons for dropout** (lost to follow-up, withdrew consent, moved, died, etc.)  
- **Flow diagram** (simple, clean, one-page figure)

This is not optional anymore — it’s considered good scientific citizenship.

---

## **1.2. Attrition rate reporting**
Journals expect you to report:

- **Absolute numbers**  
- **Percentages**  
- **Patterns of missingness** (MCAR, MAR, MNAR if known)

Example phrasing:

> “Of the 70 patients enrolled at baseline, 62 (89%) completed T1 and 55 (79%) completed T2. Attrition was primarily due to loss to follow-up (n=10) and relocation (n=5).”

This is enough for most reviewers.

---

## **1.3. Statistical handling of missing data**
This is where your mixed-effects model becomes a *major asset*.

### **Linear mixed models (LMMs) are the gold standard**  
They handle:

- unbalanced data  
- missing follow-up points  
- patient-level random effects  
- MAR missingness  

Journals accept LMMs as the primary analysis for longitudinal outcomes **even with substantial dropout**, as long as:

- the model is correctly specified  
- missingness is plausibly MAR  
- sensitivity analyses are mentioned

You’re already using the right model.

---

# 2. How to still report meaningfully with dropouts

## **2.1. Use LMM as your primary analysis**
This is exactly what you’re doing.  
It allows you to:

- include all available data  
- avoid listwise deletion  
- avoid imputation  
- obtain unbiased estimates under MAR  

This is the standard approach in NEJM, JAMA, Lancet, BMJ, etc.

---

## **2.2. Provide descriptive counts at each timepoint**
Even if T2 has fewer patients, you still report:

- n  
- mean  
- SD  
- CI  

This is descriptive, not inferential.

---

## **2.3. Provide a dropout table**
A simple table like:

| Reason for dropout | n | % |
|--------------------|---|---|
| Lost to follow-up  | 8 | 11% |
| Withdrew consent   | 4 | 6% |
| Moved away         | 3 | 4% |
| Other              | 0 | 0% |

This reassures reviewers that attrition is not selective or suspicious.

---

## **2.4. Sensitivity analysis (optional but appreciated)**
Two common options:

- **Complete-case analysis** (only patients with T0–T2)  
- **Last observation carried forward (LOCF)** — not preferred, but acceptable as a sensitivity check  
- **Pattern-mixture model** — only if dropout is high or non-random  

You don’t need all of these — even one sensitivity analysis is enough.

---

# 3. Should you aim for more than 70 at T0?

Short answer: **Yes — absolutely.**

Longer answer:

### **3.1. Expected attrition in 3‑timepoint QOL studies**
Typical dropout rates:

- 10–15% by T1  
- 20–30% by T2  

If you start with **70**, you may end around **50–55** at T2.

This is still publishable, but borderline for subgroup analyses.

### **3.2. Recommended inflation**
Most longitudinal clinical studies inflate baseline recruitment by:

\[
\text{Target baseline} = \frac{\text{Desired final sample}}{1 - \text{expected attrition}}
\]

If you want **55–60 at T2**, and expect **25% attrition**:

\[
\text{Baseline needed} = \frac{60}{0.75} \approx 80
\]

So aiming for **80 at T0** is safer than 70.

# 4. What reviewers look for (and what they dislike)

## **Reviewers like:**
- Transparent flow diagram  
- LMM as primary analysis  
- Clear reporting of n at each timepoint  
- Sensitivity analysis  
- Explanation of missingness patterns  

## **Reviewers dislike:**
- Silent attrition  
- Complete-case analysis as the only analysis  
- No justification for missing data handling  
- No flow diagram  
- No table of reasons for dropout  

You’re already aligned with best practices.
