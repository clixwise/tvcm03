# 1. Is your overall approach correct?

**Yes — absolutely.**  
You’re doing the two complementary things that any longitudinal analysis should do:

### **A. Raw descriptive summaries (df_lon1)**  
This gives you the *empirical* means, SDs, SEs, and CIs at T0, T1, T2.  
These are the “what the data say without assumptions”.

### **B. Linear mixed‑effects model (df_modl)**  
This gives you the *modeled* means, adjusting for:

- repeated measures within patients  
- patient‑level random intercepts  
- unbalanced data (if it existed)  
- shrinkage toward the population mean  

This is the “what the model estimates after accounting for correlation”.

Your model is the canonical structure for this design:

\[
\text{iter}_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{T1} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{T2} + u_{\text{patient}} + \epsilon
\]

with **random intercepts per patient**.

Given your perfectly balanced dataset (30 patients × 3 timepoints), the fixed‑effect estimates line up beautifully with the raw means.

# 2. How to obtain the *modeled means*?

Your mixed model gives:

- **Intercept = 50.003** → modeled mean at **T0**
- **T1 effect = +5.000** → modeled mean at **T1 = 55.003**
- **T2 effect = +7.500** → modeled mean at **T2 = 57.503**

These match your raw means almost exactly:

| Timepoint | Raw mean | Modeled mean |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| T0        | 50.0     | 50.003       |
| T1        | 55.0     | 55.003       |
| T2        | 57.5     | 57.503       |

This is expected because:

- the dataset is balanced  
- there are no covariates  
- the random intercepts simply shift each patient up/down but do not distort group means  

### **How to extract modeled means programmatically**

```python
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf
import pandas as pd

model = smf.mixedlm("iter_t ~ C(timepoint)", df_fram, groups=df_fram["patient_id"])
result = model.fit()

# Get modeled means
params = result.params
mean_T0 = params["Intercept"]
mean_T1 = params["Intercept"] + params["C(timepoint)[T.T1]"]
mean_T2 = params["Intercept"] + params["C(timepoint)[T.T2]"]

modeled_means = pd.DataFrame({
    "timepoint": ["T0", "T1", "T2"],
    "modeled_mean": [mean_T0, mean_T1, mean_T2]
})
```

If you want **modeled CIs**, you can use the variance–covariance matrix to compute them.

# 3. Is a forest plot applicable?

**Yes — and it’s actually a very elegant choice.**

A forest plot is ideal when you want to show:

- modeled means  
- with 95% CIs  
- across multiple timepoints  
- in a compact, publication‑ready format  

It’s especially useful when:

- you want to compare raw vs modeled estimates  
- you want to highlight the precision of each estimate  
- you want to visually emphasize the progression T0 → T1 → T2  

### What a forest plot would show

- Y‑axis: T0, T1, T2  
- X‑axis: T‑score  
- Points: modeled means  
- Horizontal bars: 95% CI  

You can even overlay raw means as hollow circles and modeled means as filled circles.

This is the kind of figure reviewers love because it’s clean, interpretable, and statistically grounded.
