In medical literature focusing on chronic venous disease (CVD), reporting VCSS and CEAP can be tricky because, unlike the VEINES-QOL/Sym (which uses a continuous T-score), CEAP is a classification and VCSS is an ordinal clinical score.

Here is how these are standardly reported in high-impact surgical and vascular journals.

---

## 1. Reporting VCSS (Venous Clinical Severity Score)

The VCSS is designed to be sensitive to change over time, making it the primary tool for measuring clinical improvement after procedures like RFA.

* **Continuous Variable Approach:** Although technically ordinal, the standard in publications is to treat the total VCSS score as a **continuous variable**.
* **Reporting:** Mean  Standard Deviation (SD) or Median (Interquartile Range, IQR) at T0, T1, and T2.
* **Comparison:** Use a Paired t-test or Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test to show the "Delta" () between T0–T1 and T0–T2.


* **The "Success" Threshold:** Some papers report the percentage of patients achieving a **clinically meaningful reduction** (often defined as a decrease of  points).

## 2. Reporting CEAP (Clinical, Etiological, Anatomical, Pathophysiological)

CEAP is a **classification**, not a scoring system. Because "C" categories (C0–C6) are categorical, you cannot average them (e.g., "an average CEAP of 2.5" is scientifically incorrect).

* **Frequency Distribution:** Report the number and percentage of limbs in each "C" category at each timepoint.
* **The "C-Downgrade":** The most common way to show evolution is a **Shift Table** or a bar chart showing the migration from higher classes (e.g., C4) to lower classes (e.g., C2) post-RFA.
* **Reporting R and L Limbs:** Since CEAP is limb-specific, you must report these separately or use "number of limbs (n)" as the denominator rather than "number of patients."

---

## 3. Standards in the Literature

There are two major frameworks you should reference to ensure your reporting meets "standard" expectations:

### The SVS/AVF Reporting Standards

The **Society for Vascular Surgery (SVS)** and the **American Venous Forum (AVF)** provide the "Gold Standard" for reporting. They suggest:

* **Primary Outcome:** Usually the change in VCSS.
* **Secondary Outcome:** Improvement in the clinical class (C) of CEAP.
* **The "Limb as the Unit":** In studies involving RFA, the standard is to report results **per limb** rather than per patient to account for bilateral disease.
